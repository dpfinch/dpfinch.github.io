Background

    Although I currently live in Edinburgh, I was born and bred in Leeds. I've always had an interest in both geography and science and decided the best thing for me to do would be a mix of the two. I enrolled at The University of Manchester to do a degree in Environmental Science and within the first month I knew I wanted to carry on studying beyond my undergraduate. My undergradute dissertation was on the formation of ice crystals in clouds at different temperatures. It was a simple experiment in a cloud chamber but it spurned me on towards a masters degree at The University of Leeds in Physics of the Earth and Atmosphere. Straight after my masters I started a PhD in atmospheric chemistry at The University of Edinburgh.

Academia

    I am currently in my 3rd year of a PhD in atmospheric chemistry at The University of Edinburgh. My research focuses on using model simulations to better understand the chemistry that occurs within smoke plumes from forest fires. The model I use is the GEOS-Chem chemical transport model (GEOS being the Goddard Earth Observing System - a weather forecast from NASA used within the model). I am interested in ozone formation when emissions from forest fires mixes with other emissions (both anthropogenic biogenic). As ozone is secondary pollutant (its not emitted directly - it is created when other chemicals emitted react with each other) understanding its formation is key in understanding air quality.

I have recently published a paper titled Origin, Variability and Age of Biomass Burning Plumes Intercepted during BORTAS-B (found here). This paper looks at how well the GEOS-Chem model can represent the carbon monoxide (CO) observed during the BORTAS campaign and where the CO came from. The paper also uses a new technique to age biomass burning plumes - an important metric in understanding the chemistry involved within the plume.
